Abstract

This research focuses on synthesizing Nickel-doped zinc ferrites using a one-step cost-effective co-precipitation method for photocatalytic remediation of chromium-contaminated water.

Key Objectives:

  • Synthesize Ni-ZnFe₂O₄ nanoparticles with enhanced catalytic properties
  • Investigate synthesis conditions on nanoparticle characteristics
  • Evaluate efficiency in reducing Cr(VI) ions in contaminated water

Chromium Contamination

A Global Environmental Challenge

Chromium in the Environment:

  • 90% of Cr emissions from industrial sources (metallurgy, textiles, tanning)
  • Two primary forms: Cr(III) (less toxic) and Cr(VI) (highly toxic)
  • USEPA limit: 100 μg/L in drinking water
  • Pakistan standard: 0.5 μg/L

"Chromium (VI) is carcinogenic if inhaled and causes liver damage, peptic failure, and affects fetal development." - WHO

Photocatalytic Degradation Mechanism

Reduction of Cr(VI) to Cr(III)

Process Steps:

  1. Cr⁶⁺ + 3e⁻ → Cr³⁺ (Reduction)
  2. O₂ + e⁻ → O₂⁻ (Superoxide formation)
  3. O₂⁻ + 2H₂O → 4OH⁻ (Hydroxyl radical formation)

Advantages: Low cost, ambient conditions, no waste generation, utilizes solar energy

Challenges:

  • Electron-hole recombination
  • Large band gap energies
  • Poor light absorption

Synthesis Methods Comparison

Literature Review Findings

Method Particle Size Advantages Limitations
Hydrothermal (Majid et al.) ~35 nm Precise stoichiometry, ultrafine particles High temperature (220°C), long duration (16h)
Co-precipitation (Shoaib et al.) ~35 nm Simple, cost-effective, uniform structure Requires careful pH control
Sol-gel (Mousa et al.) 20-30 nm Better solvation at higher temperatures Endothermic reactions
Auto-combustion (Dzunuzovic et al.) Not confirmed Suitable for pure nanosized ferrites Powder agglomeration
Citrate Precursor (Verma et al.) Small High resistivity, low dielectric losses High sintering temperature (1200°C)

Co-precipitation was selected for this study due to its simplicity, cost-effectiveness, and ability to produce uniform nanoparticles at lower temperatures.

Materials & Methodology

Materials:

Material Purity Source
Nickel nitrate hexahydrate 99.52% Sigma Aldrich
Zinc nitrate 99.89% Sigma Aldrich
Ferric nitrate 97.5% Sigma Aldrich
Deionized water 100% AC compressor

Procedure:

  1. Dissolve precursors separately in DI water
  2. Mix solutions and stir at 700 rpm
  3. Adjust pH to 10-11 with NaOH
  4. Filter precipitate and dry at 200°C
  5. Crush and calcinate at 200°C

Conclusions

Key Findings:

  1. Successful synthesis of Ni-ZnFe₂O₄ nanoparticles via co-precipitation method
  2. XRD confirms cubic spinel structure with crystallite sizes 23.61-24.13 nm
  3. Band gap tunable from 3.17 eV to 2.91 eV by varying Ni/Zn ratio
  4. FTIR confirms metal-oxygen bonds and functional groups
  5. Maximum Cr(VI) removal efficiency of 87.9% achieved
  6. Optimal conditions: 0.25g/50mL, 120 min, pH 3

Significance:

  • Cost-effective solution for water purification
  • Tunable properties for specific applications
  • Potential for industrial-scale wastewater treatment
  • Magnetic properties enable easy recovery and reuse

"The synthesized nanocomposites show great promise for photocatalytic remediation of chromium-contaminated water with efficiency comparable to existing methods but at lower cost."

Future Recommendations

Research Directions:

  • Optimize doping concentrations for enhanced photocatalytic activity
  • Investigate hybrid composites with carbon dots for reduced electron-hole recombination
  • Study reusability and stability over multiple cycles
  • Explore scale-up synthesis for industrial applications
  • Investigate performance on real industrial wastewater samples

Potential Applications:

  • Water treatment plants for heavy metal removal
  • Industrial wastewater remediation systems
  • Magnetic storage devices
  • Photovoltaic devices (due to tunable band gap)
